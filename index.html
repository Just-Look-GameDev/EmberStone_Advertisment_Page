Fragment I

"Fire does not destroy.
Fire reveals what was never strong enough to remain."

Before the world had a name, it existed as heat without direction. Not flame, not light ‚Äî but pressure. A need to become something else.

When the Emberstone awakened, it did not speak. It strained.

From that strain came land. From land came breath. From breath came thought.

The first mountains were not raised ‚Äî they were cooled. The first seas were not poured ‚Äî they were condensed. The first sky was not lifted ‚Äî it was released.

The Emberstone Prime remained at the world's heart, unseen, unheard, but endlessly burning. It did not demand worship. It did not guide hands.

It waited.

Fragment II ‚Äî On the Nature of Flame

The Forgeborn did not discover the Emberstone. They discovered that fire remembers.

When metal was forged with embers taken from deep places, the blade carried echoes ‚Äî of heat, of pressure, of purpose. When armor was tempered in ember-furnaces, it resisted not because it was strong, but because it refused to break.

The Forgeborn learned to bind memory into matter.

Cities became stories. Weapons became arguments. Architecture became belief.

And belief hardened into doctrine.

Fragment III ‚Äî The First Cities

The earliest cities were not built upward. They were carved downward, spiraling closer to the world's warmth. Every level was hotter than the last. Every descent was considered a pilgrimage.

Children were taught that to climb was to flee, but to descend was to learn.

At the lowest halls stood the Listening Forges, where ember-veins sang softly through stone. Only those who listened long enough heard patterns in the sound ‚Äî rhythms that felt uncomfortably like speech.

Some turned back. Others stayed too long.

Fragment IV ‚Äî The Ember Covenant

The Covenant did not begin as tyrants. They were caretakers.

Forge-lords who regulated ember extraction. Scholars who charted safe thresholds. Wardens who sealed unstable zones and posted warnings written in heat-resistant script.

For centuries, it worked. The world prospered, not because it was controlled, but because it was maintained.

Then came the question no one could answer:

"If the Emberstone sustains the world‚Ä¶
what sustains the Emberstone?"

No record shows who first proposed reforging it. Only that once the idea existed, it refused to die.

Fragment V ‚Äî Early Experiments

The first reforgings were small. A forest where decay ceased. A city where illness vanished. A river that flowed backward, returning what it carried.

The world did not resist. It adapted.

This emboldened them.

Ember conduits were extended across continents. The Listening Forges were converted into Command Forges. Ember-shards were embedded into living guardians, chosen volunteers who believed eternity in service was the highest honor.

They called them Wardens. None of them were built to question.

Fragment VI ‚Äî The Great Reforging

The Great Reforging was meant to be gentle. A correction. A refinement. A final tempering of reality.

But the Emberstone Prime had learned something its handlers had not:

Perfection is static. Fire is not.

When the ritual began, the world trembled not from force, but from refusal.

The Emberstone did not explode. It tore itself apart.

Fragment VII ‚Äî The Fracture

There was no single moment of destruction.

Some regions burned instantly. Others froze. Others continued unchanged for centuries, unaware they were already dead.

The sky split into layers, each holding a different echo of what once was. Cities collapsed inward, becoming labyrinths of molten stone and memory. Wardens lost their purpose but not their commands.

They continued to guard. Forever.

Fragment VIII ‚Äî On Dungeons

Dungeons are not places. They are failures that never finished happening.

Walls bend where reality tried to heal and failed. Corridors repeat because time folded to avoid collapse. Traps exist not to kill intruders, but to prevent access to things that should never be touched again.

Bosses are not masters of these places. They are prisoners.

Bound to shards that anchor unstable reality, their bodies reforged again and again until flesh and purpose became inseparable.

When they fall, the dungeon does not celebrate. It exhales.

The world should have ended. Every calculation says it should have.

As the Emberstone shattered, fragments did not scatter randomly. Many embedded themselves into living souls ‚Äî not by choice, but by proximity, by resonance, by refusal to let go.
